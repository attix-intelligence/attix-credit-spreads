3. Literature review

#PaperTake-away relevant to EXP-1710
1 Israelov, R. (2019). Pathetic Protection: The Elusive Benefits of Protective Puts. AQR / Journal of Portfolio Management. Systematic long-volatility protection on SPX is a net cost to the investor; the mirror trade (short-vol, controlled size) is a paid risk premium. Establishes the baseline that SPX option sellers are compensated — but only if position-sizing is strict.
2 Israelov, R. & Nielsen, L. N. (2015). Covered Call Strategies: One Fact and Eight Myths. Financial Analysts Journal, 71(6). Short-option-style returns decompose into long equity + short vol. Translates directly to iron condors: the "edge" is the vol-risk premium, not directional view.
3 Israelov, R. (2023). 0DTE options: A new asset class or just old wine? NDVR / working paper. Explicitly studies 0DTE SPX. Finds that ex-ante the daily vol-risk premium on 0DTE is qualitatively similar to longer-dated SPX, but slippage, skew, and discretionary execution dominate expected Sharpe. Warns that realised results depend heavily on fills.
4 Brogaard, J., Han, J. & Won, P. Y. (2023). How Does Zero-Day-to-Expiry Options Trading Affect the Volatility of Underlying Assets? SSRN 4426358. 0DTE volumes do not materially destabilise SPX; dealer hedging flows are absorbable. Good news for capacity assumptions; removes a common bear case against 0DTE premium selling.
5 Beckmeyer, H., Branger, N. & Gayda, L. (2023). Retail Traders Love 0DTE Options … But Should They? SSRN 4404704. Retail 0DTE buyers systematically lose money; by symmetry the sellers (that would be us) collect a premium. Quantifies the cross-sectional haircut to ~30–50% of gross premium after realistic frictions.
6 Bondarenko, O. & Muravyev, D. (2023). Market Return Around the Clock: A Puzzle. (Companion 0DTE work.) Shows how overnight vs intraday return asymmetries feed into 0DTE pricing; relevant to the 1DTE variant we already trade in EXP-1710.

Consensus view from the literature

  • The vol-risk premium on SPX is real and dense at the short end.
  • After realistic slippage (½–1 tick per leg) plus the cost of a disaster hedge, net Sharpe lives in roughly the 0.6–1.2 range unlevered.
  • Drawdowns are path-dependent and crowd during gap events; tail hedge is not optional.
  • Capacity at retail size ($100K–$10M) is not the binding constraint — execution quality is.

Leverage treatment

Israelov (2023) and Brogaard et al. agree that 2–3× notional leverage on a tail-hedged short-premium book is the empirical sweet spot before gap events begin to dominate expected return. This would push literature-implied Sharpe into the 1.5–2.0 band and CAGR into the 15–25% band — still an order of magnitude below the MASTERPLAN north-star CAGR of 100% and Sharpe 6.

Reality check: Even under the most generous literature assumptions, a tail-hedged 0DTE SPX iron condor book is a Sharpe-2, CAGR-20% product, not a Sharpe-6, CAGR-100% product. Framing for the MASTERPLAN: EXP-1710 is a diversifier of the EXP-1220 credit-spread engine, not a north-star replacement.
