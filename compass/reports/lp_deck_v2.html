2 · The Edge — VRP and credit-spread mechanics expanded in v2

Why VRP exists

Listed-option implied volatility persistently exceeds subsequent realized volatility — 3–5 vol points on indices, 4–8 on sector ETFs. This gap is the cost of dealer hedging in an inventory-constrained market plus a structural insurance premium end-investors pay to transfer left-tail risk.

Refs: Bakshi & Kapadia (2003); Carr & Wu (2009); BIS Quarterly Review.

Why it persists

Structural, not behavioural. Dealer balance-sheet capacity, regulatory capital charges on short-vol books (post-Volmageddon), and steady end-investor protection demand all keep VRP wide. It compresses in calm regimes, never disappears, and re-widens after every shock — including 2018-Q1 and 2020-Q1.

How a credit spread captures VRP — the mechanics

A credit spread is a defined-risk short-premium structure. For a put-credit spread we (i) sell a put closer to spot (richer in IV) and (ii) buy a further-OTM put (cheaper in IV) as protection. The package is opened for a net credit equal to the difference in option prices; the worst-case loss is bounded by the strike width minus credit.

Put credit spread — P/L at expiry
SPY · short 5% OTM put · long 8% OTM put · 30 DTE · net credit ≈ 0.40% of notional

Defined-risk payoff: profit capped at credit collected; loss capped at strike width minus credit. No assignment / tail-risk overhang.

Three independent mechanisms generate the credit

  1. Variance premium. Mean realized variance < mean implied variance over 90%+ of rolling 30-day windows since 1996.
  2. Theta decay (positive). Net short premium decays predictably; profit accrues toward expiry if underlying stays above the short strike.
  3. IV mean reversion. Elevated entry IV (we gate on VIX − RV(20) > 5%) compresses to its rolling mean post-entry, expanding the credit.

All three reinforce. The 30-DTE / 5% OTM short / 50%-of-max-profit exit (EXP-1220) captures ~70% of the textbook edge while shedding the gamma tail.

The decay math — backtest → live (EXP-2840)

Per EXP-2760 literature survey, well-executed options-selling strategies typically realize live Sharpe at 0.5×–0.7× of the backtest. EXP-2840 ran the v8a portfolio at uniform alpha-decay multipliers k on real returns:

Sharpe vs decay multiplier k
Linear-in-k decay because streams are weakly correlated (avg pairwise corr 0.018 in EXP-3230)

Break-even decay

  • k = 1.0 → headline 6.00
  • k = 0.8 → 3.86 (optimistic)
  • k = 0.6 → 2.96 (literature mid)
  • k = 0.5 → 2.49 (pessimistic)
  • k = 0.4 → ~2.00 (pause)

Break-even decay for our 3.0 "elite" floor is k = 0.61. We are publicly committed to pausing the program if 6-month rolling live Sharpe persists below 2.5.

Honest framing of the haircut

We arrive at the live range two independent ways: (i) bottom-up from EXP-2840's per-stream alpha-decay sweep (k=0.5–0.7), and (ii) top-down from EXP-2760's literature survey of analogous options strategies. Both produce 3.0–4.2. If 6-month live Sharpe is below 2.5, we pause rather than keep deploying capital — this is hard-coded as a paper-to-live gate (G2) and a Tranche-Gate condition (§6).

Honest disclosure: the 12% DD claim CAN be breached

From EXP-3200's verdict object: claim_holds_all_scenarios: false. Specifically:

  • Baseline: 2.83% of paths breach 12% (1-in-35).
  • Credit freeze (2008-style): 7.63% breach.
  • Grinding drawdown (2022-style): 97.69% breach — the claim does not hold here.
  • Vol explosion (VIX 20 → 80, 2020-style): 100% breach; mean DD 47%, worst 94%.
  • Worst p99 DD across all scenarios: 78.14% (vol_explosion, no circuit).

Our 12% target is a baseline-regime sizing target, not a guarantee. We size to it, hedge with the v5 sleeve, and circuit-break to it; we accept <18% as "tolerable but investigate", and treat anything >25% as a strategy-validity event triggering full external review and a Tranche step-down. These thresholds are written into our paper-to-live gates.

Operating rules — the execution rulebook in seven sections

The rulebook (reports/execution-optimization.md) translates the EXP-2470 / EXP-2510 / EXP-2570 measurements into a machine-checkable order construction and routing protocol. Highlights below; full rules are versioned and code-tested.

A · Hard rules (always apply)

A1
Always use complex (multi-leg) orders on the COB. Never leg. Atomic execution reduces slippage by an order of magnitude; legging risk costs 2–4¢/spread in normal vol and 10–20¢ in event windows.
A2
Trading window 10:00–15:30 ET. Reject the open and the last 30 minutes. First/last 30-min effective spreads on short-dated contracts widen ~3×.
A3
VRP gate: VIX − RV(20) > 5%. Below this threshold the historical short-vol edge collapses (Bollerslev/Tauchen/Zhou).
A4
Event proximity blackout. No new entries ±2 days around FOMC; skip earnings when implied move > 5%; skip 0-3 DTE near round strikes at triple-witching OPEX.
A5
Standard exit discipline. Close at 50% of max profit, at 21 DTE regardless of P&L, or at 2× credit lost as stop-loss.

B · Limit-pricing ladder

B1
Initial limit at mid − 0.10·Spkg for a credit spread (we're a net seller). Use COB package mid, not synthetic NBBO mid.
B2
Walk the limit on a fixed schedule toward the far quote — 5s, 10s, 20s, 35s — and abandon at 35s. Expected effective half-spread savings vs immediate cross: 35–55%.
B3
Closing trades use more aggressive k (0.35–0.50) because risk management is the priority, not opportunity cost.
B4
Marketable orders go to AIM/PIM price-improvement auctions, not market — routine 1–3¢/spread improvement.

C · Venue / routing

C1
Single best-COB routing, not sweep. SPX flow → Cboe C1; ETFs (SPY/QQQ/IWM/XLF/XLI) → broker default if Cboe-direct, else C1 → BZX/EDGX.
C2
Broker selection criteria: direct exchange access (no PFOF on options); native COB orders; AIM/PIM auction participation; per-contract pricing < $0.65.
C3
PFOF awareness. Passive limit orders — most of our flow — are the regime that PFOF routing degrades. Documented in our broker A/B (EXP-3270 proposed).

D · TCA discipline

D1
Benchmark against COB package mid at decision time + per-leg micro-mid at fill time. Never the synthetic NBBO mid (it understates complex fills).
D2
Decompose every fill into quoted half-spread, adverse selection, opportunity cost, price improvement, fees. Aggregate to a monthly TCA dashboard.
D3
Use Muravyev fair-value adjustment. Conventional half-spread overstates effective cost by ~60% when execution is fair-value-aware.
D4
Rolling 30-day stream-level effective half-spread alarm at 1.5σ above trailing baseline. Halts entries on confirmed drift before the next bar.

E · Market impact

E1
At current AUM the program operates well below the 50-contract per-spread impact threshold (Goyenko et al. 2021). Half-spread is the right thing to optimise; we re-evaluate at 25 contracts/order.
E2
Apply Almgren-Chriss to the delta book, not the option contract. Useful rule: abort if package delta-equivalent > 0.5% of underlying's 1-min volume.
E3
Don't try to time MM inventory in production. Park as research (EXP-3280 proposed).

F · 0DTE / short-DTE (if used)

F1
0-5 DTE gated to 11:00–15:00 ET — last-30-min spreads widen ~3× (Doshi et al. 2025).
F2
Respect the gamma cliff. 0DTE MM net gamma can spike to 0.04–0.17% of S&P futures liquidity in stress windows; always passive on entries, aggressive only on risk-management exits.
F3
SPX 0DTE = own benchmark. If we add 0DTE-ish streams, we build a separate effective-half-spread baseline.

We do not currently trade 0DTE; rules are in place if/when we add such a stream.
